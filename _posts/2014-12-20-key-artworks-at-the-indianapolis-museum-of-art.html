In addition to the exhibitions at the Indianapolis Museum of Art, their permanent collection is also noteworthy.

The success of an 1883 art exhibition in downtown Indianapolis (Indiana, USA), eventually turned into what is the Indianapolis Museum of Art (IMA) today. Its exhibitions are impressive: from its Ai Weiwei: According to What? exhibition (2013) to the current Georgia O’Keeffe and the Southwestern Still Life (through February 15, 2015). But so are the pieces on view throughout the museum. Three artworks in particular capture the mind and the eye: Robert Irwin’s Light and Space III (2008), Fred Sandback’s Untitled (1989) and Sol LeWitt’s Drawing No. 652 (1990).

Each piece reveals distinct artistic goals, influences and contributions to the realm of art. Yet these seemingly disparate artworks share some similarities: three American artists made pieces that call attention to space—making viewers aware of and sensitive to it—albeit in different ways.

1. Robert Irwin, "Light and Space III," 2008

Robert Irwin is a forerunner in American art and leader in the Light and Space movement, which began in Southern California (USA) during the 1960s. This movement questioned traditional art and focused on sensory perceptions. His Light and Space III is a quintessential example of a site-specific artwork at IMA that pushes conventional art boundaries.

This piece consists of floor to ceiling scrim panels with white fluorescent lights that are placed in a pattern of lines at seemingly right angles on the wall surfaces surrounding the museums’ escalators. Importantly, Mr. Irwin includes the viewer’s perceptual experience and sensory awareness, which changes depending on the relation to the piece. It can be viewed in its entirety from the Museum’s Pulliam Family Great Hall; or viewers become immersed in the work when using the escalator to travel between galleries. On a macro level, this piece also calls attention to space. In another context, such as a subway or shopping mall for instance, wall space surrounding an escalator is typically filled with movie or sale advertisements—not typically a grid of fluorescent lights. Indeed, Mr. Irwin’s use of materials and space are what make this piece so great.

2. Fred Sandback, "Untitled," 1989

Those unfamiliar with Fred Sandback’s work may initially wonder what orange yarn stretching from the gallery floor to the opposing wall may mean, or question why it is even there. Untitled demonstrates Sandback’s interest in everyday materials—metal wire, elastic cord or yarn—creating “subtly complex perceptual effects,” as The New York Times explains. What may appear as a triangle with an orange outline is merely an illusion of his real interest.

Sandback dispensed with the conventions of sculpture. He used linear outlines, diagonally, vertically or horizontally, to explore “the impression of volume and materiality,” Francis Summer explains in the book, The Grove Encyclopedia of American Art. Sandback’s yarn pieces can re-define a gallery space, which Untitled undoubtedly does. The orange color draws visual awareness to the diagonal line that cuts across the gallery space. Consequently, it redirects the viewer’s path and eye from inside the gallery towards the outside world: the opposing glass panes overlook a winter landscape. And thus, Untitled demonstrates how something so simple, acrylic yarn, conveys complex ideas about space, materiality, illusion and perception.

3. Sol LeWitt, "Drawing No. 652," 1990

Sol LeWitt, who is linked with both Minimalism and Conceptual art, is also remembered for his wall drawings. Drawing No. 652, spanning from the floor to the ceiling, is one such example. It is filled with vibrant colors and geometric shapes, and can be perceived from a variety of locations within the museum: coming down the escalator, looking over the railing on the different museum levels or exiting IMA’s Pulliam Family Great Hall.

Drawing No. 652 exemplifies one of LeWitt’s famous statements: “[The] idea or concept is the most important aspect of the work . . . The idea becomes the machine that makes the art.” This piece, which has been created and recreated, denotes this very claim. Since the original version from 1990 required a varnish that faded over time, it was recreated in 2005 and changed the original inks to acrylics. The newer version also extended the wall drawing to accommodate the larger wall surface. Both the original and current wall drawings, however, are based on the same original diagram; a team of assistants used the diagram and LeWitt’s instructions to create the piece. Thus true to his words, LeWitt came up with the idea—the work of art—that can be replicated over and over again, as IMA demonstrates.
