Richard Gray Gallery features a comprehensive exhibition of John Stezaker’s collages.

A carefully placed postcard mostly covers the faces of a posed couple. Three people make up another scene; their bodies and faces are merely outlines, filled in with layers of images. A woman’s face in a black and white portrait is entirely covered by a postcard. Each collage is distinct, but they are united by their “masked” appearance.

The artist behind these images is John Stezaker, a British conceptual artist that has used postcards, photographs and film stills in his work for decades. Stezaker’s current exhibition at Richard Gray Gallery in Chicago reveals the artist’s strength in juxtaposing images. The subsequent new forms may look quickly put together, however, they are carefully arranged.

Take “Pair XXV,” where a black and white portrait depicts a man and woman impeccably dressed and posed. The couple leans in the same direction, and perfection radiates: not even a strand of hair is out of place. Facing the viewer, their faces are partially covered with a nature-themed postcard.

This is where Stezaker’s attention to detail emerges. A sliver of the woman’s mouth and portion of the man’s face, including a part of his right eye, are left uncovered. This decision creates an unsettling sensation: a beady eye stares at the visitor. Without the postcard, the man’s eyes would probably convey happiness. Indeed the voyeuristic visitor wants to peer into the minds and lives of this young couple, but the postcard blocks a crucial means of communication—the face. Stezaker is known for configuring and reconfiguring images, thus creating new artworks with new meanings, as he does here. That said, the visitor is forced to look for other visual cues to identify the sitter and their thoughts.

Stezaker’s work is best understood by realizing how his collages differ from other artists that have used the same medium. Hannah Höch, a 20th century artist, is credited as a leader in collage and photomontage. Consider the words and fragmented images in her collage, “Cut with the Dada Kitchen Knife through the Last Weimar Beer-Belly Cultural Epoch in Germany.” Höch critiques political culture of the day and comments on gendered roles in this college and others. Stezaker has a different intent: instead of informing and drawing attention to social issues, “collage is about interrupting the seam and making something illegible,” he said in a 2014 article in The Guardian. The artist repeatedly puts these words to practice.

Images come with their own convictions. Juxtaposing one or multiple parts and pieces of various images, all equally laden with meaning, results in complex and sometimes surreal collages. In Stezaker’s “Marriage” series, for example, he splits and slices portrait images of popular movie stars, creating a “hybrid” image. In this way, the artist gives and denies information to the viewer.

Most of the current works on show conceals, wholly or partially, the person or people making up each collage, thereby redirecting the visitor’s gaze. This process differs from Stezaker’s past work. A collage like “Marriage L” vertically brings together an image showing half of a man’s face and half of a female’s into one, “new” face. This morphed figure makes eye contact with the visitor, bringing them into their transformed world. “Marriage XXVIII” follows a similar motif: two images of opposite genders are also placed together vertically. The woman’s gaze is directed at the viewer. Her piercing eye paired with the man’s clenched fist holding a cigarette creates a formidable scene. But in Stezaker’s recent constructions, the sitter’s facial expressions are oftentimes covered, indiscernible or absent. Take “Pair XXVI.” The faces of the man and woman are covered, or masked, with the exception of the woman’s lips.

Above all, Stezaker’s current collages evoke curiosity. Consider a portrait without the juxtaposition of another image or postcard: what meaning would the single image have; how is the meaning altered with the addition of an image or multiple images; which image should the viewer focus on? “The Truth of Masks” implicitly raises more questions than it provides answers. But that appears to be the point. A show about masks should not reveal everything. And it doesn’t, which is what makes Stezaker’s exhibition engaging and worth seeing.

“The Truth of Masks” is on show at Richard Gray Gallery until December 12, 2015.
